Sun Reference Wiki

Physical parameters, solar structure, and active missions

Overview

The Sun is a G2V main-sequence star at the center of the Solar System. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a gravitational collapse in a region of a molecular cloud. It contains 99.86% of the Solar System's total mass and powers all major physical and biological processes on inner planets through fusion-driven radiation.

Structure

Core: fusion zone where hydrogen converts to helium via the proton-proton chain. Temperature ~15.7 million K, density ~150 g/cm^3.

Radiative zone: energy transported outward by photon scattering. Can take 100,000 to 1 million years for a photon to traverse.

Convective zone: outermost ~30% of the Sun's radius. Hot plasma rises, cooler plasma sinks. Drives surface granulation.

Photosphere: visible "surface", ~500 km thick. Source of the sunlight we see.

Chromosphere: thin layer above photosphere, visible during total solar eclipses as a pink glow.

Corona: extended outer atmosphere, million-degree plasma. Extends millions of kilometers into space. Source of the solar wind.

Active missions (tracked in viewer)

Parker Solar Probe (NASA)
Launched 2018-08-12 | Highly elliptical heliocentric orbit
First spacecraft to "touch" the Sun's corona. Perihelion ~6.1 million km from the Sun, well inside Mercury's orbit. Uses Venus gravity assists to reduce perihelion over 24 planned orbits. Measures magnetic fields, plasma, and accelerated particles in the corona.
SOHO (ESA / NASA)
Launched 1995-12-02 | Sun-Earth L1 halo orbit
Solar and Heliospheric Observatory at the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point, ~1.5 million km from Earth toward the Sun. Continuous solar observation. Has discovered more than 5,000 comets. Provides near-real-time EUV and coronagraph imagery.
Solar Orbiter (ESA / NASA)
Launched 2020-02-10 | Inclined elliptical 0.28-1.2 AU orbit
Highly inclined heliocentric orbit using Venus gravity assists to raise inclination and image the Sun's polar regions for the first time in visible and EUV wavelengths. Complements Parker Solar Probe with remote sensing instruments where Parker only carries in-situ instruments.
SDO (NASA)
Launched 2010-02-11 | Geosynchronous Earth orbit
Solar Dynamics Observatory in geosynchronous orbit above White Sands, New Mexico. Captures continuous high-resolution EUV and magnetogram imagery. The AIA and HMI instruments image the Sun every 10-12 seconds in multiple wavelengths.
STEREO-A (NASA)
Launched 2006-10-26 | Heliocentric, slightly ahead of Earth
Solar Terrestrial Relations Observatory - Ahead. Originally one of a pair (the -B spacecraft was lost in 2014). Provides a different viewing angle of the Sun than Earth-based observatories, enabling stereoscopic study of coronal mass ejections and their Earth-directed trajectories.

Solar phenomena

Sunspots: cooler regions of intense magnetic activity. Appear dark against the photosphere. Count follows the 11-year solar cycle.

Solar flares: sudden releases of magnetic energy. Classified A, B, C, M, X (logarithmic). X-class flares can disrupt radio communications and satellite operations.

Coronal mass ejections (CMEs): large expulsions of plasma and magnetic field from the corona. Can trigger geomagnetic storms on Earth.

Solar wind: continuous stream of charged particles from the corona. Average speed ~400 km/s in the ecliptic plane.
