Parker Solar Probe coronal observations: implications for solar wind acceleration models

Review of Parker Solar Probe in-situ measurements within the sub-Alfvenic corona, with focus on the magnetic switchback phenomenon and what perihelion-pass plasma data have constrained for solar wind acceleration mechanisms.
2026-04-25 · 7 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

NASA's Parker Solar Probe (PSP) launched in August 2018 on a seven-year mission to make the closest in-situ measurements of the solar corona ever attempted. As of mid-2026 the spacecraft has executed multiple perihelion passes inside 10 solar radii, where it crosses the Alfven critical surface and samples plasma that has not yet decoupled from the Sun's magnetic field. PSP's observations have reshaped two long-standing problems in solar physics: the source of the solar wind's terminal acceleration, and the origin of strong intermittent magnetic-field reversals that pervade the inner heliosphere.

Mission profile

PSP uses seven Venus gravity assists to incrementally lower its perihelion. The first perihelion at 35.7 solar radii (November 2018) and the most recent at 9.86 solar radii (December 2024) have placed the spacecraft within solar atmospheric layers that no prior mission could probe. The four instrument suites - FIELDS for electromagnetic fields, SWEAP for thermal plasma, ISOIS for energetic particles, and WISPR for white-light imaging - operate inside a thermal shield that withstands solar fluxes of order 650 kilowatts per square metre at closest approach.

Magnetic switchbacks

The most striking early result was the ubiquitous detection of magnetic switchbacks: localised reversals of the radial magnetic-field component embedded in patches that occupy 30 to 70 percent of the encounter-phase data stream. Switchbacks were first reported by Bale et al. (2019) and Kasper et al. (2019) in Nature. They are S-shaped Alfvenic perturbations during which the field rotates by up to 180 degrees over timescales of seconds to minutes while the magnitude is approximately preserved.

Two competing classes of explanation remain active. The interchange-reconnection class proposes that switchbacks form near coronal-hole boundaries when open and closed flux-rope structures reconnect, releasing kinked magnetic flux into the wind. The turbulence-cascade class proposes that switchbacks are the natural saturated state of large-amplitude Alfven-wave turbulence and form continuously throughout the wind. Statistical patterns in PSP encounters support a mixed origin in which both processes contribute at different heliocentric distances.

Sub-Alfvenic crossings

On 2021 April 28, during Encounter 8, PSP crossed the Alfven critical surface for the first time at 18.4 solar radii. In sub-Alfvenic plasma, the wind speed falls below the local Alfven speed and the spacecraft is magnetically connected back to the Sun. Subsequent encounters have included multiple sub-Alfvenic intervals lasting hours to nearly a day. The sub-Alfvenic plasma is colder, denser, and more uniform than the super-Alfvenic wind: turbulence amplitudes drop, switchback occurrence rate falls, and the magnetic spectrum steepens. These observations validate decade-old predictions of distinct dynamical regimes inside and outside the critical surface.

Acceleration mechanism constraints

PSP data have narrowed the field of viable acceleration models. Pure thermal expansion of a hot static corona predicts terminal speeds well above the slow wind and well below the fast wind, and is not consistent with the observed bimodal distribution. Wave-driven heating from low-frequency Alfven waves can accelerate fast wind from coronal holes if reflection-driven turbulent heating dominates above the transition region. Switchback-mediated heating is a candidate for slow-wind acceleration: bursts of switchbacks correlate with elevated proton temperatures during PSP encounters, supporting models in which intermittent magnetic energy release contributes to bulk acceleration. The relative importance of these channels remains an open question and is the focus of joint PSP-Solar Orbiter campaigns now in progress.

Outlook

PSP completes its primary mission in late 2025 with three planned encounters at minimum perihelion of 9.86 solar radii. Extended-mission proposals would continue operations until propellant or thermal-protection-system limits force end-of-mission. Solar Orbiter's complementary remote-sensing observations and the upcoming MUSE and EUVST missions will fill in the spatial structure between coronal source regions and PSP's sampling points, enabling source-to-spacecraft tracking of solar-wind parcels for the first time.
