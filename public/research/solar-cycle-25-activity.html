Solar cycle 25 activity and F10.7 forecasting performance

Review of solar cycle 25 progression through 2026, the cycle's substantially-stronger-than-predicted activity record, and the operational forecasting performance of the SIDC consensus and NOAA SWPC predictions.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Solar cycle 25 began at the cycle 24 to 25 minimum in December 2019. The cycle's progression has substantially exceeded the consensus pre-cycle predictions: the smoothed sunspot number, F10.7 radio flux, and major flare counts have all run high relative to forecasts issued in 2019-2020. This report reviews the cycle 25 record through 2026, the forecast-performance comparison, and the operational implications for space-weather services.

Pre-cycle predictions

The 2019 NOAA Solar Cycle Prediction Panel issued a consensus forecast of cycle 25 reaching maximum smoothed sunspot number 115 in mid-2025, broadly comparable to or weaker than cycle 24's maximum of 116. The forecast leaned on geomagnetic-precursor methods and on the long-cycle-amplitude correlation that had been productive for cycles 20-24.

An alternative forecast by McIntosh et al. (2020) using the extended-solar-cycle framework predicted a much stronger cycle 25 with maximum smoothed sunspot number near 230. The minority forecast was based on the timing of the "terminator" event - the disappearance of the previous cycle's magnetic flux at the solar equator - and disagreed sharply with the consensus.

Observed cycle 25 progression

Cycle 25 progression has favoured the McIntosh et al. forecast over the consensus. The smoothed sunspot number passed 100 in early 2023 (a year ahead of consensus prediction), exceeded 130 by mid-2024 (already above the consensus maximum), and reached approximately 215 in late 2024 to early 2025. F10.7 reached values above 250 sfu (solar flux units) for sustained periods through 2024 - levels not observed since cycle 23 in the early 2000s.

The cycle has been notably "double-peaked" in some hemispheric metrics, with northern-hemisphere activity peaking earlier than southern-hemisphere activity by approximately one year. This north-south asymmetry is a recurring feature of solar cycles but is not predicted at cycle-onset by current forecasting methods.

Major activity events

Cycle 25 has produced substantial space-weather events. The May 2024 Gannon storm, peaking at G5 (extreme) on the NOAA storm scale on 11 May, was the strongest geomagnetic storm since the October 2003 Halloween storms. The event produced widespread auroral activity to mid-latitudes and brief operational impacts on satellite navigation accuracy and HF radio communications. October 2024 produced an X9.0 flare and associated coronal mass ejection - the largest single flare of the cycle to date.

Forecasting performance

The actual cycle 25 maximum amplitude has been near 200 percent of the 2019 consensus forecast central value. This is the largest forecast miss since the introduction of the consensus methodology in 1989. Post-event analyses by NOAA and SIDC have identified two contributing factors: (1) the geomagnetic-precursor method underweights the trans-equatorial flux transport process that the McIntosh terminator framework explicitly tracks; (2) the long-cycle-amplitude correlation methodology relies on a quasi-stationary statistical assumption that may not hold during the late phase of the modern Modern grand maximum decline.

The 2024 NOAA Solar Cycle Prediction Panel revised the cycle 25 forecast upward and committed to incorporate terminator-event diagnostics into the cycle 26 forecast methodology. The methodology revision is expected to be in place for the cycle 26 prediction issued in 2030.

Operational implications

The under-forecast cycle 25 amplitude has had material operational consequences. Low-Earth-orbit satellite drag is approximately 30 percent above the levels assumed in pre-cycle deorbit-timeline planning, accelerating natural cleanup of small debris but also shortening operational lifetimes for active spacecraft. HF communication degradation events have been more frequent than forecast. GPS positioning accuracy during major events has shown larger excursions than at any time since 2003.

Outlook

Cycle 25 is now in the declining phase, with smoothed sunspot number expected to fall below 100 by 2027-2028 and to reach minimum near 2030-2031. Continued strong activity through 2026 remains likely. Cycle 26 forecasting will benefit from the lessons learned in cycle 25 and from continued operation of the GONG and SDO observatories supplying near-real-time helioseismic and magnetic-field data.
