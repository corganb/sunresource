Solar wind structure at 1 AU as resolved by SOHO and STEREO

Review of the long-term solar wind record at 1 AU constructed from SOHO/CELIAS, STEREO/IMPACT and PLASTIC, and ACE/DSCOVR in-situ measurements, with focus on the slow / fast wind dichotomy, stream-interaction regions, and solar-cycle-dependent average properties.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The solar wind at 1 AU is observed by a small fleet of in-situ monitors providing nearly continuous coverage since the 1970s. The combination of SOHO (1995-) at the Sun-Earth Lagrange point L1, ACE (1997-) and DSCOVR (2015-) also at L1, and the twin STEREO (2006-) spacecraft in heliocentric drift orbits constitutes the modern multi-point baseline. The instrument continuity has produced a multi-cycle climatology that resolves the solar-wind structure into stable taxonomic classes and quantifies the cycle-dependent variability. This report reviews the structure as resolved by these observations.

Slow and fast wind

The first-order solar wind structure is the bimodal distribution of speeds. Slow wind (typically 300-450 km/s) emerges from the magnetic activity belt around the streamer belt; fast wind (typically 600-800 km/s) emerges from polar coronal holes during solar minimum and from low-latitude coronal holes throughout the cycle. The distinction is reflected in composition (slow wind has higher iron-to-oxygen ratio, indicating different source regions in the corona) and in fluctuation properties (fast wind has more pronounced Alfvenic fluctuations).

SOHO/CELIAS and STEREO/PLASTIC provide composition measurements (heavy-element charge states) that distinguish slow from fast wind even when the speed distinction alone is ambiguous. The compositional taxonomy is now standard in modern solar-wind analysis and is used to validate source-region attribution from corona-imaging observations.

Stream-interaction regions

Where fast wind catches up to preceding slow wind, the resulting compression-and-rarefaction structure is called a co-rotating interaction region (CIR) at solar minimum or a stream-interaction region (SIR) more generally. CIRs and SIRs are persistent features that recur at the 27-day solar rotation period and produce moderate geomagnetic activity through extended intervals of southward magnetic-field components. The structures are well-characterised in multi-spacecraft data: STEREO's heliocentric drift permits observation of the same CIR at different longitudes within the same Carrington rotation.

Solar-cycle modulation

The solar wind structure varies systematically with solar cycle. At solar minimum, fast wind dominates from polar coronal holes, slow wind is confined to a narrow streamer belt around the heliospheric current sheet, and the bimodal structure is sharp. At solar maximum, the polar coronal holes shrink, the streamer-belt slow wind expands to higher latitudes, and the solar wind is more uniformly slow with high variability. Average solar-wind speed at 1 AU varies by approximately 50-80 km/s across the cycle. Total mass flux varies by approximately 20 percent. Magnetic-field magnitude tracks the solar polar field strength and has approximately doubled at maximum versus minimum.

The grand solar minimum diagnostic

The end-of-cycle-23 to start-of-cycle-24 minimum (2008-2010) was the deepest and longest solar minimum since the start of the space age, with sustained low sunspot numbers and weak polar fields. The associated solar wind at 1 AU showed approximately 30 percent reduction in average dynamic pressure and substantial reduction in magnetic-field magnitude. The cycle 25 minimum (predicted 2030-2031) will be the next test of the cycle-dependent average-property record.

Cosmic-ray modulation

The solar wind shapes the heliospheric magnetic field that modulates galactic cosmic-ray (GCR) intensity at 1 AU. The Forbush decrease pattern (rapid GCR-flux drops following CME passages) is well-resolved in neutron-monitor data and provides a complementary tracer for transient solar-wind structure. Long-term GCR records show solar-cycle modulation of approximately 30 percent peak-to-peak in the energetic-particle component, anti-correlated with sunspot number.

Connection to the Parker Solar Probe

The Parker Solar Probe (2018-) has provided in-situ measurements at heliocentric distances down to approximately 9 solar radii during multiple perihelion passes. The Parker measurements complement the 1 AU baseline by sampling the solar-wind structure inside the Alfven critical surface, where the wind has not yet reached its quasi-steady asymptotic state. Cross-mission analyses connecting Parker observations to SOHO/STEREO 1 AU observations have refined the slow-wind generation models and identified candidate source regions for individual streams.

Outlook

The 1 AU monitoring fleet will be supplemented by ESA's Vigil at L5 (planned 2031), the Indian Aditya-L1 (operational 2024-, providing a complementary Indian Ocean longitude), and continued Parker Solar Probe and Solar Orbiter operations. SOHO is operating well past its design lifetime; ACE is operating past its design lifetime; both are scheduled for replacement by upgraded missions in the late 2020s and early 2030s. Continuity of the 1 AU baseline is treated as a strategic priority by NOAA and NASA.
